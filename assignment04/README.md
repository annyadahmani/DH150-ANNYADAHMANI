## Step 1: Specify the main design challenge 

### What is the main activity your project would support the usersâ€™ needs?
The project will support users in being able to find recipes for baking. I hope to understand how mid-aged woman find happiness when it comes to baking and how to maximize this happiness through user insight.

### What is the basic need involved for that activity?
This activity fulfills the user's basic need for happiness because baking can be seenn as a way to escape from their lives as well as a way to calm down. It also provides a community of other bakers that offers a sense of belongingness when users find other people who love to bake. By finding recipes to bake and baking them, users will be able to feel accomplished that they have gained a new baking skill or recipe to show off and use.

### What is the traditional/current solution to satisfy the needs?
The current solution to satisfy the need for baking recipes is to look online for a recipe and blindly choose one that comes up. Another current solution is to have a baking app that you can look up recipes on. And a final current solution is to have a baking book, where users physically look through the pages to find a recipe.

### What are the limitation that possibly would be improved with (digital) technology?
When using a physical baking book, people would have to look through every page or go to the table of contents to find a recipe. In addition, there is no way to customize features where people are filtering out certain ingredients when it comes to a physical baking book. People would also not be able to input what ingnredients they already have when looking at a baking book. 
Technology improves the limitations of physical baking books by allowing people to find recipes from a wider circle that would allow them to find a diverse range of baking recipes. Contrary, if people were to use baking books, they would be confined to what is in that book.

## Step 2: Target users 

### What are the characteristics of users? (ex. demographics, preference, expertise)
Demographics: people interested in baking. Specifically for this project, mid-aged women.

Preferences: users who bake as a hobby or want to get more into baking as a hobby, and therefore would want a way to find recipes efficiently. Those that find baking as a source of happiness.

Expertise: users do not need to be at any level of expertise for this project.

### Where would the users do that activity? 
To bake, users would look for baking recipes in the kitchen. To find a recipe in advance, users would likely research recipes on the Internet before going into the kitchen to start baking.

### What would the users do to complete that activity? (e.g., goal, operation, method, action)
Users would seach the internet or an app for bakinng rercipes if they were lookinng for something to bake. Additionally, users could also take the non-digital approach and look at a baking book. Users could also reach out to friends and family  about baking recipe recommendations. The ultimate goal of the users is to find an accurate recipe to what they were looking for and be able to preform what the recipe is instruncting them to do in order to create a baked treat. 

## Step 3: Pick research methods

The research method I decided to use was participatory observation. I had my interviewee do activities to give insight on her interest of baking recipes and reactions to baking websites and recipes. The interview was done in the kitchen, which is important because that is where baking is ultimately done. An iPhone was used to record the interview and the iPhone's screen recorrding was used to record the users tasks. This participant is a mid-aged woman, which fits the demographics of my project well.

## Step 6: Reflection
I thought seeing how the user thought about things was really insightful. I felt like an observer looking into what their thoughts are. I learned a lot about interviewing participants. One think I learned was that any thing that the participant says is noteworthy. Another thing I was able to learn was what an actual participant with the demographics I am looking into thinks about baking recipes. I was able to learn more about what a participant looks for in a baking website that I myself hadn't thought of. Next time, something I would do differently is having a participant that I am not related to. There would be times that my participant (my mom) would talk about something that only we know about. This wouldn't happen in a real contextual inquiry when the interviewer doesn't personally know the participant. For example, the participant references "Country Day", which was the high school I went to. The participant also mentioned people that we know in our real lives, such as my sister and a family friend who is a baker. This interview went smoothly. I was able to get insights into things I didn't think about that participants would look for in a baking website, such as the history of the dish and the importance of reviews left by users. I also was able to get insight and learn about how the geographical and cultural demographics and influences have an impact on what baking recipes a user would look for. For example, my participant is from North Africa, so a lot of her baking revolves around the recipes arround this culture. It would be interesting to look into other people from different cultures and see how culture affects peoples' recipe decisions. Although the process went smoothly, some things went differently than expected. An example of this is when my participant would anticipate my next question and would answer a question that has not been asked yet. To work around this, I would word the already-answered question differently by framing the question as a clarification to something she already said or asking her to expand on that  answer.
